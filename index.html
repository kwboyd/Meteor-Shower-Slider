• What is a meteor shower?

  • The creation of meteor showers actually starts with comets, which are large bodies of ice, dust, and rock. As a comet orbits the sun, the sun's heat melts bits of debris off the comet's surface. This debris forms a trail left in the wake of the comet.

  • When Earth passes through the comet's trail, many bits of rock and debris enter Earth's atmosphere at once. This event is known as a meteor shower.

  • Once these rocks enter Earth's atmosphere, they are considered meteors. Meteors range from the size of a dust speck to the size of a boulder, but almost all of them burn up in Earth's atmosphere before hitting the ground. During meteor showers, up to 100 meteors enter Earth's atmosphere per hour.

  • Meteor, meteorite, or meteoroid?

    Drag the rock up and down to see the differences between these terms.

    Meteoroid: a rock floating through space that has not entered Earth's atmosphere

    Meteor: a rock from space that has entered Earth's atmosphere

    Meteorite: a rock from space that did not burn up in the atmosphere and strikes Earth's surface

  • When you're looking up from Earth, the "shooting stars" that you see during meteor showers are actually the trails of white-hot air that meteors leave behind in our atmosphere as they burn.
